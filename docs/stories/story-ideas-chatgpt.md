# Twelve Graphic-Novel Story Ideas: Following the Learning Record

These short graphic-novel ideas show how one trustworthy learning record can help
four people make better decisions together:

- **Dr. Elena Ruiz**, the district learning director
- **Ms. Maya Chen**, a middle-school science teacher
- **Noah Okafor**, an intelligent-textbook designer
- **Leila Brooks**, a student in Ms. Chen's class

Rowan, the cinnamon-and-cream red panda with teal glasses, a teal neckerchief,
and a learning-record satchel, appears as a quiet guide. His question throughout
the anthology is, **“What does the evidence show?”**

The stories are fictional, but their feedback loops use capabilities described in
this textbook: xAPI records, class mastery views, prerequisite-gap analysis,
content-effectiveness reports, controlled experiments, deployment inventories,
data-quality monitoring, and privacy-aware district reporting.

## Selection Criteria

The ideas were selected for:

- **Shared benefit** — every story helps the district, teacher, designer, and student.
- **Student agency** — records start conversations; they do not replace human judgment.
- **Technical credibility** — the evidence and reports match the LRS specification.
- **Visual drama** — each plot turns an invisible learning pattern into a visible clue.
- **Variety** — the collection covers instruction, content design, operations, equity,
  privacy, experimentation, and recovery.

## Recurring Character Sheets

Use these sheets as the visual source of truth for the first three stories and for
every later story in this anthology. Copy the character's **prompt anchor** verbatim
into every image prompt in which that character appears, then append only the pose,
expression, and action needed for that panel. Do not change a character's age, face,
hair, body type, signature colors, glasses, or recurring accessories between panels.
Clothing may change only when the setting requires it; retain the character's
signature color and silhouette when it does.

All five characters should be drawn in the same warm contemporary educational-comic
style: clean ink contours, softly painted color, expressive but natural faces, gentle
paper texture, and realistic proportions with slightly simplified features. Avoid
photorealism, caricature, chibi proportions, and branded clothing or devices.

### Background-Character Rule

Unnamed students may vary across panels only if they are not meant to represent the
same learner. When a background classmate recurs within a story, give that classmate
one simple invariant (for example, a purple headband or a green checked overshirt) and
repeat it in every relevant prompt. Do not add names, disabilities, family details,
academic labels, or emotional diagnoses that the story does not establish.

### 1. The Red Node Before Monday

**[Read the complete 8-panel story →](the-red-node-before-monday/index.md)**

| | |
|---|---|
| **Setting** | Friday afternoon, Cedar Grove Middle School |
| **Theme** | A weak prerequisite can hide beneath a difficult new concept. |
| **Connection** | Class Mastery Heatmap, Struggle Detector, and Prerequisite Gap Analysis |

Leila keeps missing ecosystem questions even though she studies every night. Ms.

*Why this inspires:* A red mark becomes a useful clue rather than a label of failure.

### 3. Two Paths Through the Storm

**[Read the complete 9-panel story →](two-paths-through-the-storm/index.md)**

| | |
|---|---|

*Why this inspires:* Curiosity becomes an experiment, and an experiment becomes a
better experience for the next learner.

| | |
|---|---|

## A Shared Visual Language

If these ideas become full graphic novels, use a warm contemporary educational-comic
style with clear expressions, readable dashboard shapes, and a teal, cinnamon, cream,
navy, and amber palette. Keep the four recurring characters visually consistent.
Use the canonical character sheets and prompt anchors above in every panel prompt;
do not paraphrase away their identifying features.
Rowan should point to evidence, connect people, or lower anxiety; he should never act
as a decorative mascot or announce a decision the human characters have not examined.

Recurring visual motifs can bind the anthology together:

- a glowing thread that follows an xAPI record from Leila's textbook to each role;
- concept nodes changing from amber to teal as understanding develops;
- Rowan opening his satchel to reveal records as cards, never student secrets;
- four windows onto the same evidence at student, class, content, and district scale.

## How to Develop an Idea

To turn an idea into a complete illustrated story, invoke the available
`story-generator` skill with its title and suggested panel count. For example:

> /story-generator The Red Node Before Monday --panels 8

The full story should include a cover, the numbered panels, concise narration, and
self-contained 16:9 image prompts. Preserve the central rule of this collection:
**the record supplies evidence, people supply context and judgment, and the next round
of evidence checks whether their decision actually helped.**
